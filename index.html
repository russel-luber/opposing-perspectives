Proposition: Officers with more complaints face less accountability.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Added a diagonal “perfect accountability” reference line, setting an ideal baseline. (Score: 1)
  • Plotted all officers with semi-transparent points to emphasize density and structural failure. (Score: 1.5)
  • Annotated outliers with many complaints but few confirmed violations, highlighting extremes. (Score: 1)

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Binned officers into complaint quintiles and computed average confirmed violations. (Score: 1)
  • Chose bar chart to present upward trend visually, implying increasing accountability. (Score: 1)
  • Used neutral ColorBrewer palette to project professionalism and readability. (Score: 1)

Final Reflection

This project made me realize how subtle design choices can dramatically shift the framing of a visualization. Even neutral-seeming decisions like adding a reference line or choosing a threshold embed specific expectations about what “accountability” should look like. Designing two opposing visualizations forced me to see how the same dataset can plausibly support contradictory interpretations.

One surprise was how easily “earnest” charts could still be highly persuasive without resorting to misleading practices. However, I also noticed how annotation, binning, and color framing naturally push readers toward certain conclusions. Ethical visualization, I learned, is less about avoiding persuasion altogether and more about signaling your framing honestly. Transparency about structure and aggregation choices is essential to maintaining fairness, especially on controversial topics like policing.
