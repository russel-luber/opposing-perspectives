Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Russel Luber — rluber@ucsd.edu

Proposition: Officers with more complaints face less accountability.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Plotted all officers as dots and added a diagonal line representing perfect accountability \(one confirmed violation per complain\)
    • The line assumes that every complaint should be substantiated, which is not necessarily a reasonable expectation. It imposes a moral baseline rather than an institutional one. In reality, there are such things are false reports of misconduct.
  • I used transparent points and full axis scales to emphasize the volume of low-accountability cases.
  • Annotated outliers with many complaints but few confirmed violations, highlighting extremes.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Binned officers into complaint quintiles and computed average confirmed/substantiated violations.
  • Chose bar chart to present upward trend visually, implying increasing accountability. (Score: 1)
  • Used neutral ColorBrewer palette to embody professionalism and readability.

Final Reflection

This project made me deeply aware of how much framing and aggregation choices shape what a viewer takes away from a visualization. There really is just a garden of forking paths when it comes to design choices as a data analyst and visualizer. The same underlying data — in this case, NYPD officer complaints — can be used to argue opposing conclusions based on how it's grouped, binned, or annotated.

I found that even decisions I considered "neutral" in the moment introduced a framing effect. It became clear that rhetorical power often hides in unquestioned conventions.
